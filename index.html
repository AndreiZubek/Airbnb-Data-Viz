Data Visualization Project Group 70

An Analysis of Airbnb Pricing in New York City

This project looks at how location and room type affect Airbnb prices across New York City. We want to see where prices are highest, where they stay lower, and how those patterns change from one borough to another.

Introduction

Airbnb prices in New York City are not spread evenly. A listing in Manhattan does not compete in the same market as a listing in the Bronx, and an entire apartment is priced differently from a private or shared room. Looking at these differences helps us understand how location and room type shape the market.

This topic matters because Airbnb sits in the middle of tourism, travel costs, and local housing. If we can see the pricing patterns more clearly, hosts can make better decisions, travelers can compare options more realistically, and policymakers can look at how short term rentals vary across the city.

Introduction to the Data

Dataset used: Kaggle Link

Data size: The original dataset has 48,895 rows. After cleaning and filtering, we used 5,000 rows for the visualizations on this page.

Main attributes: The dataset includes listing and host identifiers, borough and neighborhood, latitude and longitude, room type, price, minimum nights, review counts, review frequency, host listing counts, and yearly availability.

Room types: Entire home/apt, Private room, Shared room.
Boroughs: Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx, Staten Island

Visualization 1: Average Price by Borough

Interaction instructions: Use the dropdown inside the chart to switch between all listings, entire homes, private rooms, and shared rooms. Hover over a bar to see the exact average price.

Takeaway: Manhattan has the highest average prices in every view. The outer boroughs are usually cheaper, especially for private rooms and shared rooms.

Visualization 2: Price Patterns by Borough and Room Type

Interaction instructions: Hover over a cell to see the borough, room type, listing count, and price values. Use the toggle to switch between average price and median price.

Takeaway: Looking at borough and room type together makes the price gaps easier to spot. The median view also helps show where a few expensive listings may be pulling the average upward.

Visualization 3: Price Distribution by Room Type

Interaction instructions: Hover over each room type to see its quartiles, median price, and listing count. The legend above the chart shows how to read the box plot.

Takeaway: Entire homes tend to cost more and vary more in price. Private rooms stay lower overall, and shared rooms sit at the bottom of the price range.

Visualization 4: Availability by Borough and Room Type

Interaction instructions: Hover over a cell to see the borough, room type, listing count, and average availability over the year. The legend below the chart explains what the colors mean.

Takeaway: Availability adds a different angle to the pricing story. Some parts of the city keep listings open much longer, which may point to different host strategies or different demand patterns.

Visualization 5: Geographic Price Distribution

Interaction instructions: Use the dropdown in the top left to filter listings by borough. Hover over any dot to see the listing name, neighborhood, room type, and nightly price. Zoom and pan to explore dense areas like Manhattan and Williamsburg up close.

Takeaway: Manhattan continues to dominate as the most expensive borough, with listing along the west side consistently appearing in the darkest red. Brooklyn shows the widest price range of any borough, with neighborhoods near Manhattan, like Williamsburg and DUMBO, being noticeably more expensive. On the other hand, listings further east are much more affordable. Queens and the Bronx are quite budget-friendly, with most listings falling under $100/night. It's interesting to see that Staten Island has very few listings compared to the other boroughs, reflecting its limited role in the NYC short-term rental market.

Conclusion

Overall, location is the strongest driver of Airbnb pricing in New York City, and room type adds another clear layer of difference. The charts also show that price is only part of the story. Availability and price spread matter too. In future work, we could look more closely at neighborhood-level patterns, reviews, or seasonal trends.
