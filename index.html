Data Visualization Project Group 70

An Analysis of Airbnb Pricing in New York City

This project looks at how location, room type, neighborhood, and reviews shape Airbnb prices across New York City. We wanted a mix of views that show both the broad patterns and the details hiding inside them.

Introduction

Airbnb prices in New York City are not spread evenly. A listing in Manhattan does not compete in the same market as a listing in the Bronx, and an entire apartment is priced differently from a private or shared room. Looking at these differences helps us see how the city’s market changes from place to place.

This topic matters because Airbnb sits in the middle of tourism, travel costs, and local housing. If we can see the pricing patterns more clearly, hosts can make better decisions, travelers can compare options more realistically, and policymakers can look at how short term rentals vary across the city.

Introduction to the Data

Dataset used: Kaggle Link

Data size: The original dataset has 48,895 rows. After cleaning and filtering, we used 5,000 rows for the visualizations on this page.

Main attributes: The dataset includes listing and host identifiers, borough and neighborhood, latitude and longitude, room type, price, minimum nights, review counts, review frequency, host listing counts, and yearly availability.

Room types: Entire home/apt, Private room, Shared room.
Boroughs: Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx, Staten Island

Visualization 1: Geographic Price Distribution

Interaction instructions: Hover over a point to see the listing name, price, room type, and review count. The colors show price categories from budget to luxury.

Takeaway: The most expensive listings cluster in Manhattan, with another concentration in parts of Brooklyn. The outer boroughs are much lighter overall.

Visualization 2: Price Distribution by Room Type

Interaction instructions: Hover over each room type to see the quartiles, median price, and listing count. The legend above the chart explains how to read the box plot.

Takeaway: Entire homes are pricier and more spread out. Private rooms and shared rooms sit lower, and their price ranges are tighter.

Visualization 3: Average Price by Neighborhood

Interaction instructions: Hover over a bar to see the neighborhood, borough, listing count, and average price. The bars are sorted by price, and the colors separate the boroughs.

Takeaway: The priciest neighborhoods are mostly in Manhattan, but the borough colors also show smaller pockets of higher prices elsewhere.

Visualization 4: Pricing Patterns in Guest Engagement

Interaction instructions: Left chart (density heatmap): Hover to see clusters of listings by price and review count. Darker = more listings. Right chart (bars): Hover to see average and median reviews for each price range.

Takeaway: Price isn't the only factor that drives reviews. Budget listings ($0–$100) actually accumulate more reviews on average, which suggests that frequent travelers book budget options more often. High prices don't really have an impact on a user's decision to leave a review. Instead, convenience and location seem to be more important factors.

Visualization 5: Borough and Room Type Analysis

Interaction instructions: Use the dropdown above the chart to switch between average price and average availability views. Hover over a cell to see the borough, room type, listing count, and the selected metric value. The color scale below the chart summarizes the pattern.

Takeaway: This heatmap shows important patterns across NYC's Airbnb market. Manhattan and entire homes consistently have the highest prices, while shared rooms remain the most affordable option citywide. Availability tells a different story: entire homes are booked more frequently (lower availability), suggesting higher demand, while private rooms offer more consistent availability across boroughs.

Conclusion

Overall, location is the biggest driver of Airbnb pricing in New York City, but room type, neighborhood, and review activity all add useful context. The charts show that price is not just one number. It shifts by place, by listing type, and by how the market is behaving around each listing. In future work, we would look more closely at seasonality, reviews, and neighborhood-level patterns.
